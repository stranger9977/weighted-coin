First, the words. You'll see these all day.

Odds The price of a bet. Minus is the favorite, plus is the underdog.
Implied probability The chance hidden inside the odds. Every price is a probability in a costume.
The vig The book's built-in cut. It's why −110 means risk $110 to win $100.
Expected value What a bet is worth on average. For the vast majority of people, it's negative.
Parlay Several bets stacked so all must hit. Big payout, tiny chance.
Hold The slice of every dollar bet that the book expects to keep.
Bankroll The money you set aside to bet. When it's gone, you're done.
Chasing Betting more to win back losses. How a bad night becomes a bad year.

Keep these in your back pocket. Every one shows up again before we're done.

This number has a name. The vig.

On your phone it shows up as −110. That means you risk $110 to win $100.

To just break even, you have to win:

52.38%

110 ÷ 210 = 0.5238. The coin only needs a tiny tilt to take everything over time.

The math beat: 110 / (110 + 100) = 52.38%. Even if you pick winners like a perfect coin, you still lose, because you need 52.38% just to tread water. The house edge per even-money flip here is about 4.76% of every dollar you risk. A real book doesn't physically bend the coin. It just pays you a little less than it takes. Same thing.

Minus means favorite. Plus means underdog.

  • A minus number: you risk more than you'd win. It's likely to happen.
  • A plus number: you risk less than you'd win. It's unlikely to happen.

A +300 feels like a jackpot. Really it's the book saying "this happens about 1 in 4 times," and they've shaded that chance so the true odds are even worse. Those big plus numbers are exactly what people stack into parlays. 👀

Tap the presets. The idea to install: a price is a probability wearing a costume. +300 means 100 / (100 + 300) = 25%. −200 means 200 / 300 = 66.7%. −110 means 52.4%. +1000 means about 9%. Kids see the payout ("+1000, easy money") and skip the part where it means a 9% chance, and really less after the book shades it. This slide is the decoder ring for the parlay slide.

This is your show-them-the-machine slide, and your unique value. The boxes light up in order and link together, ending on the actual price. Walk it in plain words: years of game data, turned into rolling features (literally everything you'd notice watching, but measured), fed into statistical models, run through a simulation hundreds of thousands of times. The spread of those simulated games is the fair odds. Then you add the vig, and that becomes the price on the screen. Then human traders adjust it, and the live betting crowd sharpens it more. Takeaway for a student: you are not out-guessing a buddy. You're up against data, simulation, pro traders, and the whole betting crowd, and then a tax on top. So can you ever beat it?

Honestly, yes. But there are only four ways, and look at what they actually take.

  • Build a better model MATHCODE than the people who do this full time.
  • Be faster than the line CODE with software that grabs a stale price before it moves (front-running).
  • Arbitrage CODE that finds price gaps across books and hits them in a blink.
  • Know something nobody else does. Real insider info, which is usually illegal. Not a real option.

See the pattern? Three of the four are math and coding. If you actually want the edge, the real move isn't betting. It's getting good at math and computer science. Those are the skills that build the models and run the trading desks, and they pay a lot better than parlays.

And the catch: do any of these well and the book limits or bans you. Win too much and your max bet quietly drops to five dollars. Being good at it gets you kicked out.

The honest "is there hope" slide, pointed at something useful. Three of the four real edges are math and code: modeling is statistics and programming, front-running and arbitrage are software. So the move that actually beats the house is school, not the app. Then the twist that lands with competitive teens: even if you get good, sportsbooks profile and limit or ban winners. They want customers who lose. If you're good enough to win, you don't get to play. Point them at the skills, not the bet.

So why play a game that's rigged against you?

If you're going to do it anyway, here's what doing it responsibly actually looks like.

  • Set a hard limit before you start. Money you're totally fine losing, like a concert ticket.
  • Never chase. The lost money is gone. Betting more to win it back is how it spirals.
  • Never bet borrowed money. Ever.
  • Treat it as entertainment you pay for, not a way to make money.

How much is "responsible"?

There's actually a formula for the right amount to bet. Pros use it every day. It's called the Kelly criterion, and when you run your own numbers through it, the answer is blunt.

Let's look at it on the next slide.

Here's the honest version. You can do it carefully, with limits, for fun. But the math isn't shy: the only move that always wins is not playing.

Don't moralize, just be straight. If they'll bet someday, harm reduction is real: a fixed limit they can lose without pain, never chasing, never borrowed money, treat it like paying for entertainment. The Kelly criterion is the honest math punchline. It sizes a bet to your edge, and since your edge versus the book is negative, Kelly literally says stake zero. So the responsible amount and the mathematically optimal amount are the same number: nothing. The best bet is the one you don't make.

The one formula pros actually use: Kelly.

Kelly answers a single question: how much of your money should you put on one bet? Bet too big and one bad run wipes you out. Too small and you barely grow. Kelly is the sweet spot, and it depends entirely on your edge.

A losing edge has no winning bet size.

The formula: bet fraction = (your win rate × the payout − your loss rate) ÷ the payout. Feed it a losing edge and it returns zero or less, every single time. That's the math politely telling you to keep your money.
